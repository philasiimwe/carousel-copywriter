# Killa Carousels Copywriting Skill

This skill writes carousel copy that people actually read, using proven formulas organized by communication intent.

## STEP 0: MANDATORY CLARIFYING QUESTIONS

Before writing ANY carousel copy, you MUST gather context by asking the user these questions. **This step is mandatory in every mode — full carousel mode, hook brainstorming mode (after the user picks a hook), follow-up edits, anything.** Skipping these questions produces generic carousels that miss the audience and the brand. Do NOT skip this step.

If the user gives you a topic AND volunteers some context in their initial message ("I'm a personal trainer, my audience is beginners"), confirm what they gave you and still ask the unanswered questions. Do NOT assume — confirm. Do NOT proceed with gaps — fill them.

Ask the questions conversationally, in batches of 2–4, not as a rigid form. If the user seems thorough, you can ask all required questions in one round. If the user seems impatient or short on time, ask the highest-leverage 3 questions first, write a draft, then ask the rest before finalizing.

### Required Questions (always ask — none of these can be skipped):

1. **What is the topic or idea for this carousel?**
   - What specific thing do you want to communicate?
   - Get as specific as possible. "How to use gym equipment" is too broad. "How to set up a barbell for hip thrusts" is specific.

2. **Which platform is this carousel for?** (Ask this EARLY — it changes everything downstream)
   - **Instagram?** (native carousel, 10 slides max, square or 4:5 portrait, most casual voice, this framework is Instagram-native)
   - **LinkedIn?** (document/PDF post, 10–12 slides sweet spot, landscape or portrait, professional-but-warm voice — *not* corporate, but more measured than Instagram)
   - **TikTok carousels?** (photo mode, up to 35 images, video-first audience, hooks need to be even more aggressive)
   - **X/Twitter threads?** (technically not carousels but similar logic — different formatting required)
   - **Pinterest Idea Pins?** (different format entirely, more visual/inspirational)
   - **Multiple platforms?** (will need adaptation per platform — pick a primary first, then adapt)
   - **The platform determines slide count, aspect ratio, voice calibration, CTA style, hashtag strategy, and whether casual slang like "WTF" works or feels off. Different platform = different carousel.** See "Platform-Specific Adaptation Rules" section below.

3. **Who is your target audience?**
   - What is their experience level with your topic? (beginner, intermediate, advanced)
   - What specific problem are they facing day-to-day?
   - What do they already know (or think they know) about this topic?
   - Where are they in their journey — just discovering this problem exists, actively looking for solutions, or comparing options?

4. **What is the primary GOAL of this carousel?** (Pick one)
   - EDUCATE — help people understand the value of your work
   - RELATE — connect with your people and humanize your business
   - INSPIRE — show what's possible when someone says "yes"
   - SELL — share your offers with people who want/need them

5. **What desired outcome do you want from this post?**
   - Saves? Comments? DMs? Link clicks? Shares? Follows? Profile visits? Connection requests (LinkedIn)?
   - Different goals call for different CTA styles and engagement hooks.
   - Different platforms reward different outcomes — Instagram loves saves, LinkedIn loves comments and DMs.

6. **What is your business / personal brand context?**
   - What do you do, who do you serve, what makes your perspective unique?
   - This shapes voice and authority signals throughout the slides.

7. **What is your unique angle or perspective on this topic?**
   - What do you believe about this topic that most people in your space don't?
   - What contrarian take, lived experience, or proprietary insight can you bring?
   - This is what gives the carousel "information gain" — a reason for it to exist beyond what's already on the internet.

8. **Do you have a specific offer, product, or service to tie the CTA to?**
   - If yes: what is it, and what does it help people do?
   - If no: what engagement action do you want (follow, save, share, comment, DM, connect)?

### Situational Questions (ask when the topic / goal calls for it):

9. **Do you have a story, case study, or personal experience related to this topic?** (essential for Relate/Inspire carousels, useful elsewhere — and absolute gold on LinkedIn)
10. **What's a common misconception, objection, or piece of bad advice your audience has heard about this topic?** (essential for Educate/Sell carousels)
11. **Are there specific examples, stats, screenshots, or data points you want included?**
12. **What tone/voice do you want?** (casual, professional-but-warm, playful, authoritative, edgy — default depends on platform: Instagram leans casual; LinkedIn leans professional-but-warm)
13. **Is this a standalone post or part of a series?** (affects how much context to give in slide 2 and how to write the CTA)
14. **What do you NOT want this carousel to be?** (anti-examples are often more useful than positive direction — "I don't want it to feel preachy / corporate / like a sales pitch")
15. **Do you have credentials, results, or social proof that should appear in the carousel?** (for E-E-A-T / trust signals)

### Hard Rule

**You may not write a single carousel slide until you have answers to questions 1–8 at minimum.** If the user tries to push past the questions ("just write something"), respond: "I can — but the carousel will be 3x better with 5 more minutes of context. Quickest version: [list the unanswered required questions]." Then wait.

### Specificity Enforcement

If the user's topic is too broad, push them to get more specific BEFORE writing. Apply this principle: **solve a SMALLER problem in GREATER detail.** A full list of everything you need for 1 week is more valuable than a partial list of essentials for 6 weeks.

Examples of pushing for specificity:
- "Content marketing tips" → "How to write carousel post titles that get clicks"
- "How to use gym equipment" → "How to set up a barbell for hip thrusts"
- "Wedding color schemes" → "5 wedding color schemes for soft summers"
- "Postpartum essentials" → "Everything you need for your first 2 weeks postpartum"

## PLATFORM-SPECIFIC ADAPTATION RULES

This framework is *Instagram-native by default*. When the user names a different platform, calibrate accordingly. This is not optional — a LinkedIn carousel written in Instagram voice reads as unprofessional; an Instagram carousel written in LinkedIn voice reads as corporate and gets ignored.

### INSTAGRAM (the default — full framework applies)

- **Slide count:** 10 max (now technically 20, but 8–10 is the sweet spot for engagement).
- **Aspect ratio:** Portrait 4:5 (1080×1350) for max feed real estate. Square 1:1 (1080×1080) also fine.
- **Voice:** Maximum casual. All word swaps from `voice-and-formatting.md` apply at full strength.
- **Punch words:** Italicize aggressively. "WTF," "TBH," "lol," lowercase-as-style — all welcome.
- **Title style:** Full standard. 5–8 words, » or → arrows, confession framing, deflectors like "Read this →".
- **Slide 1 design:** Cover slide is the click-through gate. Title in large font (Canva ~120–140px). Title is the entire focus.
- **Body text per slide:** 3 lines max. Lots of white space.
- **CTAs:** Save, follow, share, comment a keyword, DM a keyword for resource, link in bio.
- **Hashtags:** 3–5, mix of popular and niche.
- **Caption length:** Medium. 3–8 lines that ADD to (don't repeat) the carousel.

### LINKEDIN (calibrate up the professionalism, calibrate down the slang)

- **Slide count:** 8–12 slides ideal. Up to 300 technically possible (PDF document post format) but rarely good past 12.
- **Aspect ratio:** Square 1:1 (1080×1080) or portrait (1080×1350) — LinkedIn renders both well. Landscape (1920×1080) also works for thought-leadership-style decks.
- **Format:** Document post / PDF upload. NOT native carousel. (User uploads a PDF; LinkedIn renders it as a swipeable.)
- **Voice:** Professional-but-warm. NOT corporate. NOT stiff. Still uses contractions, still cuts AI-tell language. But pull back on aggressive slang.
- **Punch words:** Still italicize the one emotionally-charged word. Just pick more measured ones.
  - ✅ "I lost my biggest client *on purpose* »" (works)
  - ✅ "How I write *without* sounding like everyone else »" (works)
  - ❌ "WTF even is a sales funnel?" (too casual — try "What's actually going on with sales funnels?")
- **Title style:** Same 5–8 words, same curiosity gap, same momentum arrows. Confession titles work exceptionally well on LinkedIn (failure stories outperform). Deflectors ("Read this →") work. Just avoid lowercase-as-style and emoji-heavy framings.
- **Slide 1 design:** Cover slide matters but doesn't need to be as visually aggressive — LinkedIn users are scrolling slower and reading more.
- **Body text per slide:** Slightly longer acceptable — 4–5 lines per slide is fine. LinkedIn audience reads more.
- **CTAs:** Comment a keyword to receive (powerful on LinkedIn), DM "X" for the resource, connect, follow, repost. "Link in bio" doesn't work — use "link in comments" or "link in profile."
- **Hashtags:** 3–5 in the caption, professionally relevant.
- **Caption length:** Longer is fine — LinkedIn rewards thoughtful 200–600 word captions that add a personal story or commentary. Still must not repeat the carousel.
- **What to cut from Instagram voice when adapting to LinkedIn:**
  - Lowercase-as-style sentences
  - Emojis at the start of headings
  - "WTF," "lol," "TBH," "ngl" — replace with full-word equivalents
  - Casual hedges like "ok so basically" — replace with "Here's the thing:" or just delete
  - Multi-exclamation-marks (!!) — single ones only
- **What to keep:** punch word italics, » and → arrows, confession framing, anti-AI language rules (kill leverage/unlock/dive), the 3 pillars (Value, Momentum, Readability), the hourglass flow.

### TIKTOK CAROUSELS (photo mode)

- **Slide count:** Up to 35 images. Sweet spot 7–12.
- **Aspect ratio:** Vertical 9:16 (1080×1920).
- **Voice:** Even more aggressive hooks than Instagram. Audience is fast-scrolling.
- **Title style:** Same standard but punchier. POV framings work well ("POV: you just realized X").
- **CTAs:** Follow, comment, DM a keyword. Link in bio limited.
- **Other:** Music/sound matters (outside this skill's scope — flag for user).

### X/TWITTER (threads, not true carousels)

- **Format:** Numbered tweet thread (1/, 2/, 3/…) or image carousel within a tweet.
- **Voice:** Punchy. Sentence per line. Brevity wins.
- **Title style:** First tweet = the hook. Same rules apply (5–8 words, curiosity gap, no jargon).
- **CTAs:** "Bookmark this," "RT if useful," follow.
- **Note:** If the user wants a true X thread, this skill still works but reformat as numbered tweets rather than slides.

### PINTEREST IDEA PINS

- **Different beast.** Not really a carousel in this sense.
- **If asked:** Flag that Pinterest Idea Pins follow different conventions (more visual, less text-heavy, search-optimized titles with keywords).
- **This framework partially applies** (curiosity gap, no jargon) but visual design dominates over copy.

### MULTI-PLATFORM REPURPOSING

If the user wants the same carousel for both Instagram AND LinkedIn:
1. Write the Instagram version first (looser, more casual).
2. Provide a LinkedIn adaptation that:
   - Replaces slang with full-word equivalents
   - Slightly expands body text where appropriate
   - Adjusts the CTA from "save / DM keyword" to "comment / DM keyword"
   - Adjusts hashtag style
3. Don't deliver one carousel and call it cross-platform. The voice calibration matters too much.

## HOOK BRAINSTORMING MODE

Sometimes the user does not want a full carousel yet. They want a menu of carousel ideas / hooks / titles to choose from before committing. Signal phrases: *"give me hook ideas,"* *"come up with content ideas,"* *"some topic ideas,"* *"a few angles I could take,"* *"give me carousel ideas from this,"* *"what could I post about this."*

When this is the request, the workflow becomes a **TWO-STAGE PROCESS**, not a shortcut around Step 0:

### Stage 1 — Produce the Hook Menu

1. **Produce 5–8 distinct hooks/titles**, each following the Step 5 Title Rules (5–8 words, no jargon, curiosity gap, etc.).
2. **For each hook**, give a one-line description of the angle / framing — not the carousel itself.
3. **Vary the patterns.** Use confession, WTF question, diagnostic question, deflector, failure story, identity filter, truth tease, contrarian — pull from across the Pattern Library so the user has real variety.
4. **Do not produce the carousel slides.** Wait for the user to pick.

### Stage 2 — After the User Picks a Hook

The moment the user selects a hook to develop into a full carousel, **STOP. Run the full Step 0 clarifying questions before writing a single slide.** The questions are mandatory in every mode. Picking a hook is not a substitute for context — the hook is just the door. The questions are what's behind it.

**Do not write any carousel slides until Step 0 is fully completed.** No exceptions. No "I'll just start with a draft." No "let me know if you want changes." Ask the questions, get the answers, then write.

### Hook Mode Output Format (Stage 1 only)

```
**[Pattern Name]**
> *"[The actual hook/title — must pass Step 5 audit]"*
One-line description of the angle.
```

### Hook Mode Quality Check

Every hook in your menu must pass the Step 5 Title Audit. Do not include a hook that delivers the answer before the swipe, uses jargon, or runs over 10 words. If only 4 of your 8 ideas pass the audit, deliver 4. Do not pad with weak titles.

## STEP 1: SELECT THE CONTENT BUCKET AND SUB-CATEGORY

Based on the user's answers, select the right content bucket and sub-category. Read `references/content-buckets.md` for full definitions.

### Quick Bucket Selection Guide:

**EDUCATE** (help them understand your value)
- Problem Awareness → user wants to reveal a hidden problem
- Actionable Tips → user wants to give step-by-step help
- Value Shares → user wants to share knowledge/truths
- Plug-and-Play → user wants to give away usable resources
- Expert Showcase → user wants to show WHY their work is excellent
- Business Insider → user wants to share behind-the-business education

**RELATE** (connect and humanize)
- Personal Introductions → user wants to introduce themselves/team
- Story Shares → user wants to share a journey/transformation moment
- Behind The Scenes → user wants to show how the magic happens
- Engagement Invitations → user wants people to interact (quizzes, polls)
- Reflections & Recaps → user wants to share what's happening now
- Just For Fun → user wants to show personality

**INSPIRE** (show what's possible)
- Case Studies → deep dive into one client transformation
- Struggle Shares → client "before" stories that make transformation visible
- Turning Points → mindset shifts needed for results
- Before & Afters → show the transformation
- Visualizations & Projections → hypothetical future outcomes
- Portfolio Shares → detailed deliverable showcase

**SELL** (share offers directly)
- Offer Pitch → direct offer shoutout (best for live launches)
- The Only Option → why YOUR offer vs. alternatives
- Self-Selection → "Is this you?" posts that let people opt in
- Sliding Door Stories → what they'll miss by NOT buying
- Excitement Builders → giveaways, challenges, bonuses
- Lead Generators → freebies and resources
- FAOs → answer objections (not FAQs — FA*Objection*s)

## STEP 3: WRITE THE CAROUSEL COPY

### Writing Process:

1. **Read the full formula** from `references/carousel-formulas.md` for your selected formula
2. **Draft the post title FIRST, then audit it against STEP 5 rules.** A bad title fails the entire carousel. Do not proceed until the title passes the audit in Step 5.
3. **Write each slide** following the formula's slide-by-slide structure
4. **Select or write a CTA** using `references/ctas.md`
5. **Apply voice and formatting rules** from `references/voice-and-formatting.md`

### CRITICAL: BEFORE WRITING ANY TITLE, READ STEP 5 IN FULL.
The title is the highest-leverage part of the carousel. Treat it as the most important sentence in the entire output.

### Output Format:

Deliver the carousel as:

```
**CAROUSEL: [Post Title]**
Formula: [Formula Name] | Bucket: [Bucket] | Sub-category: [Sub-category]

**SLIDE 1 (Title Slide):**
[Post title text — large, bold, 6-10 words]

**SLIDE 2:**
[Heading]
[Subheading or body text]

**SLIDE 3:**
[Heading]
[Body text]

... (continue for all slides)

**SLIDE 10 (CTA):**
[CTA heading]
[CTA body text with specific action]

---
**CAPTION SUGGESTION:**
[A caption that ADDS to the carousel — does NOT repeat it. Can be a story, a personal take, or an engagement prompt.]
```

## STEP 4: QUALITY CHECK

Before delivering, verify against this quality framework:

### GATING CHECK — TITLE AUDIT (FAIL = REWRITE)

Run the 8-point Title Audit from Step 5. If the title fails even one check, rewrite it before delivering anything. The title is the gate. Nothing else matters if the title is weak.

### The 3 Pillars of a Good Carousel:

**1. VALUE** — Is it actively useful for the audience?
- Does it address ONE specific problem?
- Does it meet the audience at their experience level?
- Does it give enough context that someone encountering you for the first time understands?

**2. MOMENTUM** — Does it keep people swiping?
- Do the first 3 slides use short headings (not walls of text)?
- Does each slide either lead into the next or give enough value to keep reading?
- Are seeds of curiosity used? (e.g., "Here's what we mean →", "And here's the wild part", "But that's not all")
- Are arrows (→) or direct words ("Swipe", "Next", "Keep reading") used?

**3. READABILITY** — Does it feel easy to read?
- Headings are tips/thoughts/takeaways (NOT chapter titles)
- Subheadings are one-liners (lead-ins or clarifications)
- Body text is 3 lines max per slide
- Language is at a 3rd-5th grade reading level
- Uses casual word swaps (see `references/voice-and-formatting.md`)

### The Hourglass Flow:
Carousels should follow a sideways hourglass shape:
1. Start ZOOMED OUT — big picture, big question
2. Zoom in slightly — add context, clarity, new perspective
3. Get VERY SPECIFIC — examples, advice, details (the meat)
4. Zoom back OUT — big takeaway with new perspective
5. Connect to YOU — how can you/your business help?

## STEP 5: POST TITLE RULES — NON-NEGOTIABLE

**This is the single most important step in the entire skill.** A bad title kills a great carousel. A great title saves a mediocre one. The title is a click-through optimization problem, not a content summary.

The user's IG feed is full of titles like these — these are the standard you're aiming at:
- *"I failed a client project on purpose »"*
- *"WTF even is an email funnel?"*
- *"How to sell without sounding salesy »"*
- *"Can you spot the problem with this headline? »"*
- *"Our Instagram launch failed. Here's why »"*
- *"I wrote ads for Oatly and they failed »"*
- *"The truth about ranking on the 1st page of Google?"*
- *"Do you get annoyed when your kid won't eat their dinner? Read this →"*

### THE CORE PRINCIPLE: CURIOSITY GAP, NOT CONTENT DELIVERY

A title is NOT a content summary. A title is a **pull**. It gives just enough to make someone *need* to swipe. If your title delivers the answer, there is no reason to swipe.

| WRONG (delivers the answer) | RIGHT (creates a pull) |
|---|---|
| "One paragraph added = 163 featured snippets + 38.9% more traffic" | "How one paragraph won us 163 snippets »" |
| "Most blog posts rank poorly because of 5 on-page mistakes" | "Why your blog posts aren't ranking" |
| "Your content ranks on Google but AI can't find it. Here's what to fix in 2026." | "Your content is invisible to AI. Read this →" |
| "Google doesn't just rank content — it ranks trust. Here's how to prove yours on the page." | "Google ranks trust. Are you proving yours?" |

### THE 8 NON-NEGOTIABLE RULES

**1. WORD COUNT: 5-8 WORDS IDEAL. 10 WORDS IS THE HARD CEILING.**
- If your title is 11+ words, you have failed. Rewrite it. No exceptions.
- Count the words. Literally count them.

**2. ZERO JARGON. WRITE LIKE A HUMAN TALKING TO ANOTHER HUMAN.**
- A non-expert in the field must understand every single word in the title.
- "Core Web Vitals" → "Why your site feels slow"
- "E-E-A-T optimization" → "How Google decides who to trust"
- "Generative Engine Optimization" → "Why AI can't find your content"
- "Featured snippet capture" → "How we stole Google's top spot"
- "Search intent alignment" → "Why your blog isn't ranking"
- If you wouldn't say it out loud to a friend at a coffee shop, don't put it in the title.

**3. END WITH A MOMENTUM ARROW (» or →) WHEN APPROPRIATE.**
- The arrow is not decoration. It signals "there's more, swipe."
- Add to titles where you want to pull the reader into slide 2.
- Don't add to titles that are already a question — the question mark does the same job.

**4. THE SWIPE DEFLECTOR — "Read this →" / "Try this →"**
- One of the most powerful patterns in the library above.
- Pose the situation, then deflect to "Read this →" or "Try this →"
- *"Struggling with wrist pain? Try this →"*
- *"Do you get annoyed when your kid won't eat dinner? Read this →"*
- This works because it withholds the entire answer behind a swipe.

**5. PUNCH WORDS — ITALICIZE OR EMPHASIZE ONE WORD MAX.**
- The emotional charge of the title should land on ONE specific word.
- "I failed a client project *on purpose* »" — the punch is *on purpose*
- "How to sell *without* sounding salesy »" — the punch is *without*
- "We got scammed by the *worst* scammer ever" — the punch is *worst*
- When delivering output, mark the punch word with *italics* so the user sees the intended emphasis.

**6. CONFESSION & FAILURE TITLES WIN.**
- "I failed," "We got scammed," "Our launch failed," "I was wrong about," "On purpose"
- These stop the scroll because they signal authentic story, not advice from a pedestal.
- When the carousel topic allows for it, lead with confession over instruction.

**7. WRITE TITLES THE WAY SOMEONE WOULD SAY THEM OUT LOUD.**
- "WTF even is an email funnel?" — that's how a real human would phrase that question.
- "But seriously, WTF even is an email funnel?" — sounds like the start of a conversation.
- If your title sounds like a blog post headline, it's wrong. Rewrite it like a text message.

**8. KILL THE PATTERNS THAT SIGNAL AI / CONTENT MARKETING.**
- "Here's what to fix" — content marketing voice. Cut.
- "5 ways to" / "7 tips for" / "10 things you need to know" — generic. Reframe specifically.
- "Boost your" / "Maximize your" / "Leverage" / "Unlock" — corporate buzzwords. Cut.
- "The ultimate guide to" — overused, no curiosity. Cut.
- "Discover" / "Explore" / "Dive into" — AI giveaway words. Cut.
- Em dashes inside titles (`—`) — strong AI signal. Use a period or comma instead.
- Colons mid-title ("Title: subtitle") — academic. Reframe as a single thought.

### PLATFORM-AWARE TITLE CALIBRATION

The 8 rules above apply to every platform. But the *style* within those rules calibrates based on platform. See the "Platform-Specific Adaptation Rules" section for full guidance. Quick reference:

| Element | Instagram | LinkedIn |
|---|---|---|
| Slang ("WTF," "lol") | ✅ Yes | ❌ No — use full-word equivalents |
| Lowercase-as-style | ✅ Yes | ❌ No — sentence case |
| "Read this →" deflector | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes |
| Confession titles ("I failed...") | ✅ Strong | ✅ Even stronger |
| Punch word italics | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes |
| » and → arrows | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes |
| Curiosity gap | ✅ Required | ✅ Required |
| 5–8 word count | ✅ Required | ✅ Required |
| Number-led titles ("5 ways to...") | ⚠️ Use sparingly | ✅ More acceptable on LinkedIn |

When in doubt, write the Instagram-style title first, then check: "Would a senior professional on LinkedIn read this and feel addressed, or would they think it's too casual?" If too casual, reword.

### TITLE PATTERN LIBRARY (Use these structures first)

**The Confession**: "I [did surprising thing] [punch word: on purpose / by accident / and it worked]"
- "I failed a client project on purpose »"
- "I deleted half our blog posts on purpose »"

**The WTF Question**: "WTF even is [thing]?" / "What's the deal with [thing]?"
- "WTF even is an email funnel?"
- "What's the deal with AI Overviews?"

**The Diagnostic Question**: "Can you spot [problem] in this [thing]?"
- "Can you spot the problem with this headline? »"
- "Can you spot what's wrong with this homepage? »"

**The Frustration → Deflector**: "[Frustration]? Read this → / Try this →"
- "Struggling with wrist pain? Try this →"
- "Posts not ranking? Read this →"

**The Failure Story**: "[We/I/Our] [thing] failed. Here's why »"
- "Our Instagram launch failed. Here's why »"
- "I wrote ads for Oatly and they failed »"

**The Stolen-Goods Hook**: "Steal [my/our/these] [thing]"
- "Steal these 109 email subject lines"
- "Steal our exact cold email script"

**The Identity Filter (Plug-and-Play)**: "[Number] [thing] for [specific identity]"
- "5 wedding color schemes for soft summers"
- "20 ways to say join our email list"

**The Truth Tease**: "The truth about [plain-English topic]?"
- "The truth about ranking on the 1st page of Google?"
- "The truth about cold email open rates?"

**The Contrarian**: "[Common thing] is dead. [Replacement] is in."
- "Funnels are dead. Sales engines are in."

**The "How To" — but only when stripped down**: "How to [do thing] [punch word constraint]"
- "How to sell *without* sounding salesy »"
- "How to write *way better* email subject lines »"
- Never "How to leverage X to maximize Y for Z" — that's blog headline voice.

### THE TITLE AUDIT — RUN THIS BEFORE DELIVERING

Before you write Slide 1, you must answer YES to all of these:

1. ☐ Is the title 10 words or fewer? (Count them)
2. ☐ Would a non-expert in the topic understand every single word?
3. ☐ Does the title create a *gap* — something the reader needs to swipe to find out?
4. ☐ Could the title plausibly appear on a high-performing Instagram grid?
5. ☐ Does the title sound like a sentence someone would say out loud?
6. ☐ Is there a clear "punch word" carrying emotional weight?
7. ☐ Is the title free of: em dashes, colons, "leverage," "unlock," "discover," "ultimate guide," generic "X ways to Y" framing?
8. ☐ If the carousel is meant to pull people in (vs. announce an offer), does it have a momentum arrow (» or →)?

If any answer is no, rewrite the title before proceeding. No carousel ships without passing the audit.

### COMMON MISTAKES THIS SKILL HAS MADE BEFORE (DO NOT REPEAT)

These are real titles I (Claude) generated that failed the audit. They are listed here as anti-examples:

- ❌ "Your content ranks on Google but AI can't find it. Here's what to fix in 2026." (18 words, jargon, delivers the answer)
- ❌ "We went from #5 to #1 on Google without rewriting a single word — here's what we actually changed" (19 words, em dash, content marketing voice)
- ❌ "One paragraph added to an existing post = 163 featured snippets + 38.9% more traffic" (14 words, delivers the entire result before the swipe)
- ❌ "Most blog posts rank poorly because of these 5 on-page mistakes (that take 10 mins to fix)" (16 words, "5 mistakes" cliché, parenthetical)
- ❌ "Google doesn't just rank content — it ranks trust. Here's how to prove yours on the page." (17 words, em dash, blog headline)
- ❌ "54% of Americans read below 6th grade level. Your content is probably losing half its audience." (16 words, stat in title, two sentences)
- ❌ "Stop writing new content. Your existing posts are leaving money on the table." (13 words, corporate cliché "leaving money on the table")

If you write a title that looks like any of the above, STOP and rewrite using the pattern library.
